About

I didn't learn economics
in a classroom.
I learned it surviving.

I'm Aman — a student from Bihar, India, belonging to a Scheduled Tribe community. I grew up in a home where the refrigerator was switched on only when necessary, where we slept on a mattress on the floor because we couldn't afford a bed, and where every rupee was accounted for before it was spent.

I didn't know the term "opportunity cost" then. But I was already living it. That curiosity turned into a blog, a classroom, and a commitment — to understand the systems that shape lives like mine, and eventually, to change them.

I write about the economics that happens outside lecture halls: in slum markets, in family budgets, in government school corridors where teachers never show up. Economics as it is actually lived — by the people it most affects.

"My journey with economics didn't begin in a classroom — it began on the floor of a shared room, in a home where every rupee mattered."

— Aman · The 100 Rupees Economist
The 100 Rupees Classroom

A free classroom born from
the system's failure.

For two years, I've been teaching economics and business basics to 30 students aged 14–16 from Patna's low-income communities — most of them school dropouts or students in government schools with no functioning teachers.

📍
Patna, Bihar
Operating out of a community space serving students from low-income families and slum communities — the kids most likely to fall through the cracks.
📚
What We Teach
Economics fundamentals, business basics, financial decision-making — concepts designed around real choices these students and their families face daily.
🎯
Why It Exists
Because government schools in Bihar are failing. Because coaching costs money nobody has. Because someone has to show up — so I did.
